Step 3: Alert Triage

Quickly classify alerts as True Positive, False Positive, or Needs Investigation
What Is Triage and Why Is It Critical?

Triage is the process of quickly evaluating alerts to determine their validity and priority. In a busy SOC, analysts may receive hundreds of alerts per shift. Without effective triage, the team drowns in noise and misses real attacks.

The Three Triage Categories
CategoryColorMeaningAction
True Positive (TP)GreenAlert accurately detects a real threatEscalate to investigation immediately
False Positive (FP)GrayAlert triggered but no real threat existsDocument and tune the rule to reduce noise
Needs InvestigationYellowInsufficient information to classify yetGather more data and escalate
Triage Decision Flow
1. Is this alert from a known-bad source (IP, domain, hash)? → TP
2. Does the alert match expected behavior or maintenance? → FP
3. Is more context needed (check other logs, ask the asset owner)? → Investigate
4. Does this alert correlate with other alerts happening at the same time? → TP (likely part of an attack chain)
Tip: In this exercise, all alerts in our dataset are True Positives — they are part of a real attack chain. In a real SOC, the FP rate can be 70-90%! A good SOC aims to reduce false positives by fine-tuning detection rules.
Triage Best Practices
  • Work critical alerts first — attackers don't wait
  • Don't spend more than 5-10 minutes on a single alert during triage; if you can't classify it, mark "Investigate" and move on
  • Use multiple log sources to confirm — a web alert + firewall alert + sysmon alert for the same IP = strong TP signal
  • Document everything — your triage notes will be used later in investigation and reporting
